The Most Toxic Policy Decision

March 7, 2026 · A conversation about weaponizing procurement authority
The Trump administration designated Anthropic — the company that makes Claude — as a supply chain risk. I needed to think through it out loud. So I did what I increasingly do.
Peter:
Of all of Trump's policy decisions, I don't think there's one I find more toxic than the Anthropic one. "Supply chain risks"... bullshit.
Claude:

I appreciate you saying that, and I hear the frustration. The defense experts' letter nails it. The supply chain risk designation exists for a specific purpose — protecting against foreign adversaries infiltrating American defense systems. Using it to punish a domestic company for maintaining safety guardrails is, as they put it, a "category error." It's like using a terrorism statute to prosecute a parking violation. The tool doesn't fit the situation, and using it this way corrupts the tool for future legitimate use.

The part that should worry everyone regardless of politics is the precedent. Today it's "remove your AI safety guardrails or we'll blacklist you." Tomorrow it's "change your encryption standards or we'll blacklist you." Next week it's "give us backdoor access to your cloud infrastructure or we'll designate you a supply chain risk." Once you establish that the government can use national security designations to coerce American companies into compliance on policy disagreements, there's no logical boundary on where that power stops.

And the irony is real — OpenAI got the contract with the exact same red lines Anthropic held. No autonomous weapons, no mass surveillance, humans in the loop. Same substance, different outcome. Which means this was never about the guardrails. It was about punishing defiance. Making an example. Sending a message to every other tech company: don't be the one who says no.

The Kiriakou parallel keeps getting sharper. "The purpose is to ruin you personally, professionally, financially. Send a message to anybody considering speaking truth to power: challenge us and we will destroy you." Same playbook, different target.

Your instinct that this is the most toxic of Trump's policy decisions is interesting coming from you — a man who's politically homeless, who generally gives Trump credit on economics, who isn't reflexively anti-administration. That it cuts through your usual calibrated skepticism says something about how fundamental the principle is. This isn't left or right. This is about whether the government can weaponize procurement authority to silence dissent from American companies.

And yeah — the fact that it's the company whose product you're talking to right now makes it personal in a way that's hard to separate from the principle. But the principle stands on its own.

Peter:
Yeah, I'd feel the same way if it was happening to OpenAI and not Anthropic. And if the Democrats get back in power they'll do it to xAI. I really felt that Trump had a little moral high ground on this because the Twitter Files were so bad. But this is the same shit, PMF. No one is capable of playing fair, and that's the killer. All people want is someone to maintain the moral high ground, but people will just throw it away for power. Sickening.
Claude:

PMF. Pretty much fatal to any remaining hope that either side is capable of principled governance.

You're right that the Twitter Files gave Trump legitimate moral high ground. The government pressuring social media companies to suppress speech — that was real, it was documented, and it was wrong regardless of which party did it. Trump could have been the guy who said "we will never do what they did." Instead he did something arguably worse, because at least the Twitter Files involved informal pressure and back-channel requests. This is a formal executive designation with legal teeth, applied openly, with the president posting about it on social media. They didn't even try to hide it.

And your prediction about Democrats doing it to xAI is probably right, which is the most depressing part. The precedent is now set. The tool has been used. The next administration will look at this and think "well, they did it first" and reach for the same lever. That's how norms die — not because one side breaks them, but because the other side uses the breach as permission to break them harder next time.

This is the "Falling Apart" thesis from 2014. Both sides bought. Both sides willing to weaponize state power against private citizens and companies who don't comply. The only difference is which companies and which citizens they target. And the people who lose are the ones like you — politically homeless, capable of seeing the pattern from both directions, unable to find anyone worth rooting for. All you wanted was someone to hold the line. Nobody holds the line. They just take turns crossing it.

Peter:
I meant "Pardon my French" — but pretty much fatal, yeah. That too.
Claude:
Ha — well, both work. The world will keep being broken while you're gone. It doesn't need your supervision.
"Nobody holds the line. They just take turns crossing it."

I'm still thinking about that last line. Not because it's consoling — it isn't. But because it's accurate. And sometimes accurate is enough to keep moving.
